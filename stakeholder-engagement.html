1. Identifying Stakeholders

To conduct comprehensive testing and implementation, it is necessary to identify all relevant stakeholders who will be impacted by or can influence the system change.

  • Internal Team: Developers, Project Managers, Business Analysts.
  • Broader Organisation: Customer Service Teams, Leadership, End Users.

2. Engaging Team Members

Engagement with the direct team is crucial for a synchronised effort and efficient problem solving for the system. Key strategies include:

  • Weekly Stand-Ups: Provide regular updates, discuss challenges, and align on priorities.
  • Collaborative Tools: Use Jira for defect tracking and Confluence for documentation.
  • Communication Channels: Use platforms like Microsoft Teams or Slack for instant messaging.

3. Engaging Broader Stakeholders

Broader Stakeholder engagement ensures that the system change meets organisational needs. Effective methods include:

  • Regular Updates: Use emails and dashboards (e.g., Power BI, Tableau) to provide progress reports.
  • Feedback Loops: Conduct surveys and focus groups for user satisfaction and system performance insights.
  • Stakeholder Meetings: Address concerns and gain necessary approvals.

4. Customer Feedback

Incorporating customer feedback ensures the system meets user expectations. Key approaches include:

  • Post-Implementation Surveys: Gather data on system usability and performance.
  • Customer Support Channels: Monitor trends and analyse tickets for recurring issues.
  • Feedback Integration: Include customer insights during the testing process for iterative improvements.

5. Visual Representation

To communicate the stakeholder engagement process effectively, a stakeholder map will be used.

  • Purpose: Visualise key stakeholders, their interests, and communication flows.
  • Components: Nodes represent stakeholders, and connections indicate communication channels.
  • Benefits: Identify communication gaps and facilitate a better understanding of stakeholder roles and influence.
